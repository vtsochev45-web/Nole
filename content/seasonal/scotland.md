# Farming in Scotland: Crop & Livestock Opportunities (+ Grant Map)

Scotland’s unique climate, soils, and topography create both challenges and opportunities for farmers. From the fertile lowlands of the Central Belt to the rugged Highlands, Scotland offers diverse agricultural possibilities supported by a range of government schemes.

This guide covers everything you need to know about farming in Scotland: climate zones, best crops, livestock options, current support schemes, a seasonal planning calendar, and where to find local advice and funding.

---

## 1. Scottish Agricultural Climate Zones

Scotland’s climate is **temperate maritime**, meaning it’s mild for its latitude but wet, windy, and highly variable. The North Atlantic Drift keeps winter temperatures relatively mild, while summer temperatures are cooler than southern Britain. However, rainfall and sunshine vary dramatically across the country.

| Zone | Characteristics | Typical Regions |
|------|----------------|-----------------|
| **West Coast & Islands** | Mild winters, cool summers, very high rainfall (up to 4,577 mm/year), strong winds. Limited arable potential but excellent for hardy livestock. | Highlands, Argyll, Outer Hebrides |
| **East Coast Lowlands** | Drier (less than 870 mm/year), more sunshine, colder winters, warmer summers. Scotland’s prime arable land. | Lothian, Fife, Grampian, Tayside, Borders |
| **Central Lowlands** | Moderate rainfall, milder temperatures, sheltered valleys. Mixed farming: cereals, potatoes, dairy, beef. | Glasgow–Edinburgh corridor, Ayrshire, Stirling |
| **Southern Uplands** | Higher elevation, cooler, wetter than east but less extreme than west. Sheep farming dominates. | Dumfries & Galloway, Scottish Borders |
| **Highlands** | Harsh climate, short growing season, high rainfall, poor soils. Mainly rough grazing for sheep and deer. | Cairngorms, Northwest Highlands |

**Key climate factors:**
- **Rainfall:** West receives up to 4,577 mm/year; east often under 870 mm/year.
- **Sunshine:** East and southwest coasts see 1,471–1,540 hours/year; Highlands as low as 711–1,140 hours/year.
- **Temperature:** Average annual temperatures range from ~7°C (north) to 9°C (south). Frost risk persists from October to April inland.
- **Wind:** North and west experience over 30 gale days/year.

These zones directly dictate what you can grow and rear profitably.

## 2. Best Crops for Scottish Conditions

Scotland’s soils are diverse, but often acidic, coarse-textured, and poorly drained. The east’s fertile soils are among the world’s most productive for cereals.

### Cereals & Oilseeds
- **Barley:** Scotland’s most important cereal (340,000 ha). Spring barley dominates (malting, feed). Varieties suited to shorter growing season, cooler summers.
- **Wheat:** Grown on better lowland soils (87,000 ha). Mainly milling wheat for bread-making.
- **Oats:** Traditional Scottish crop (32,000 ha). Tolerates wetter, cooler conditions; demand growing for health foods.
- **Oilseed Rape:** Expanding in east (34,000 ha). Needs well-drained soil, careful disease management.

### Potatoes & Vegetables
- **Potatoes:** Major crop (29,000 ha). East Scotland produces seed and ware potatoes for UK market.
- **Soft Fruit:** **Strawberries** (911 ha under cover) – Scottish strawberries prized for flavour. Also raspberries, blueberries.
- **Brassicas:** Kale, cabbage, swede – traditional fodder crops; some for human consumption.
- **Carrots & Turnips:** Grown on lighter soils in east.

### Forage & Fodder
- **Grass & Silage:** Perennial ryegrass, clover mixes. High rainfall supports high yields in west.
- **Maize:** Limited to sheltered south-west (Ayrshire) for silage.
- **Field Beans & Peas:** Nitrogen-fixing legumes improving soil health.

**Top tip:** East = arable & potatoes; West = grass & forage.

## 3. Livestock Opportunities

### Beef Cattle
- **Suited to:** All regions, especially uplands and less productive land.
- **Breeds:** Aberdeen Angus, Highland, Luing, Shorthorn, Hereford cross.
- **Systems:** Suckler beef (cow-calf), finishing on grass or cereal-based rations.
- **Market:** Premium Scotch Beef PGI label commands higher prices.
- **Considerations:** Hardiness needed for outdoor wintering in north/west.

### Sheep
- **Suited to:** Uplands, hills, islands – Scotland has 6.6 million sheep.
- **Breeds:** Scottish Blackface (hill), Cheviot, North Country Cheviot, Suffolk, Texel.
- **Systems:** Hill farming (breeding ewes), lowland finishing (store lambs).
- **Market:** Scotch Lamb PGI; strong export demand.
- **Considerations:** Predation (foxes, eagles), harsh weather, labour intensity.

### Dairy
- **Suited to:** South-west (Ayrshire, Dumfries & Galloway), central lowlands.
- **Breeds:** Holstein-Friesian, Jersey cross, Ayrshire (historic).
- **Systems:** Mostly intensive year-round housed or grass-based seasonal.
- **Market:** Fresh milk, cheese (Scottish cheddar, Isle of Mull, Orkney).
- **Considerations:** High capital investment, volatile milk price.

### Other Livestock
- **Pigs:** Outdoor pig production (saddleback) in south.
- **Poultry:** Free-range eggs, broilers – niche markets.
- **Deer & Game:** Venison production expanding; grouse moors (sporting estates).
- **Aquaculture:** Salmon farming in west coast sea lochs, trout in freshwater.

**Livestock numbers** have been declining but remain a cornerstone of Scottish agriculture.

## 4. Scottish Government Support Schemes

Scotland administers its own agricultural support through **Rural Payments and Services** (RPID). Post‑CAP, the Scottish Government is transitioning to a new **Agricultural Support Framework**.

### Current & Upcoming Schemes
| Scheme | Purpose | Key Details |
|--------|---------|-------------|
| **Basic Payment Scheme (BPS)** | Direct income support (phasing out). | Apply by 15 May; payments from December. |
| **Agri‑Environment Climate Scheme (AECS)** | Environmental improvements, biodiversity, carbon sequestration. | Multi‑year agreements; capital works (hedging, wetland creation). |
| **Forestry Grant Scheme** | Woodland creation, management, timber production. | Up to 80% of costs; supports agroforestry. |
| **Crofting Agricultural Grant Scheme (CAGS)** | Support for crofters (small‑scale, community‑based farming). | Grants for fencing, drainage, livestock handling. |
| **Sustainable Agriculture Capital Grant Scheme (SACGS)** | Investing in equipment, technology, efficiency. | Up to 50% of costs (max £50,000). |
| **Knowledge Transfer and Innovation Fund (KTIF)** | Training, innovation, benchmarking. | Workshops, consultancy, pilot projects. |
| **Farming for a Better Climate** | Voluntary climate‑action measures. | Soil testing, nutrient management, carbon audits. |
| **Scottish Rural Development Programme (SRDP)** | Broad rural business development. | Now largely replaced by above schemes. |

**Important:** Always check the [Rural Payments and Services website](https://www.ruralpayments.org) for the latest deadlines, eligibility, and application windows.

### Grant Map (Simplified)
- **Highlands & Islands:** Focus on crofting, forestry, environmental schemes.
- **East Lowlands:** Arable, potato, and horticulture grants; water management.
- **South‑West:** Dairy, livestock, woodland creation.
- **Uplands:** Agri‑environment, peatland restoration, sheep support.

## 5. Seasonal Planning Calendar

A month‑by‑month guide to key farming activities in Scotland.

| Month | Arable | Livestock | General |
|-------|--------|-----------|---------|
| **Jan‑Feb** | Soil sampling, seed ordering, winter crop checks. | Lambing prep, feed budgeting, housing checks. | Grant applications (BPS, AECS). |
| **Mar‑Apr** | Spring sowing (barley, oats), potato planting, fertiliser application. | Lambing, calving, turnout to grass. | Fencing, drainage works before nesting season. |
| **May‑Jun** | Cereal herbicide/pesticide applications, early silage cuts. | Shearing, weaning, pasture rotation. | Agri‑environment scheme works commence. |
| **Jul‑Aug** | Winter barley harvest, straw baling, oilseed rape swathing. | Store lamb sales, bulling/heifer selection. | Show season (Royal Highland Show). |
| **Sep‑Oct** | Main cereal harvest (wheat, spring barley), potato lifting. | Housing preparation, autumn grazing management. | Soil sampling, cover crop sowing. |
| **Nov‑Dec** | Winter wheat/barley drilling, ploughing, field repairs. | Housing livestock, winter feeding plans. | Payment window opens (BPS, other schemes). |

**Regional variations:** West coast livestock turnout later (April/May); east coast harvest earlier (August).

## 6. Local Resources

### Advisory & Research
- **SRUC (Scotland’s Rural College):** Advice, training, research. [www.sruc.ac.uk](https://www.sruc.ac.uk)
- **James Hutton Institute:** Soil, crop, environment science. [www.hutton.ac.uk](https://www.hutton.ac.uk)
- **Soil Association Scotland:** Organic certification, support.
- **NFU Scotland:** Farmer union, lobbying, business services. [www.nfus.org.uk](https://www.nfus.org.uk)

### Funding & Grants
- **Scottish Government Rural Payments & Services:** [www.ruralpayments.org](https://www.ruralpayments.org)
- **Scottish Enterprise:** Business growth, innovation grants.
- **Highlands and Islands Enterprise:** Regional development support.
- **Forestry and Land Scotland:** Woodland creation advice.

### Markets & Cooperatives
- **Quality Meat Scotland (QMS):** Promotes Scotch Beef, Lamb, Pork. [www.qmscotland.co.uk](https://www.qmscotland.co.uk)
- **Scottish Dairy Association:** Dairy sector representation.
- **Scotch Whisky Association:** Barley market linkage.
- **Local marts:** Stirling, Lanark, Thainstone, Dingwall.

### Training & Events
- **Royal Highland Show (June):** Major agricultural show, innovations.
- **ScotSheep, Winter Fair:** Specialist events.
- **SRUC short courses:** Business, technical skills.

## 7. Conclusion: Key Takeaways

1. **Know your zone:** East = arable, west = livestock.
2. **Barley is king:** Scotland’s top cereal; malting premium available.
3. **Sheep dominate uplands:** Blackface, Cheviot hardy breeds.
4. **Beef suits most regions:** Aberdeen Angus & Highland cattle thrive.
5. **Dairy concentrated in south‑west:** High input, high output.
6. **Grants are transitioning:** Keep up with Scottish Government schemes.
7. **Seasonal timing is critical:** Short growing season demands precision.
8. **Local advice pays:** SRUC, NFUS, Hutton Institute are invaluable.

Scotland’s farming future is balancing productivity with environmental sustainability. With the right mix of crops, livestock, and support, Scottish farming can be both profitable and resilient.
